《CC:A Conceptual Perspective》Some notes of Chapter2

发表于 2018-05-06

Two formulations of P-vs-NP question

  • Search problem vs Decision problem: definition and relation
  • two classes of Search problem: $\mathcal{PC}$ and $\mathcal{PF}$, “Easy to find solutions” vs “Easy to check the correctness of solutions”.
  • two classes of Decision problem: $\mathcal{P}$ and $\mathcal{NP}$. “Easy to prove” vs “Easy to verify the proof/witness”
  • Important: $\mathcal{NP=P} \iff \mathcal{PC \subseteq PF}$
    • There are some problems in $\mathcal{PF}$ that are not in $\mathcal{PC}$! That is, there are some problems that are easy to find solutions but hard to check the correctness of given solutions.
    • $\mathcal{NP=P} \iff \mathcal{PC \subseteq PF}$
  • Another definition of $\mathcal{NP}$: non-deterministic polynomial-time Turing machine; NP-proof system and NTM.

Reductions

  • Cook-reduction: Using oracle
  • Karp-reduction:
    • A poly-time computable function f is called a Karp-reduction of $S$ to $S’$ if, for every $x$,it holds that $x \in S$ iff $f(x)\in S’$.
    • For decision problem; Transformation of problem instance.
  • Levin-reduction:
    • A pair of poly-time computable functions, f and g, is called a Levin-reduction of R to R’ if:
      • f is a Karp-reduction of $S_R=\{x:\exists y \text{ s.t. } (x,y)\in R \}$ to $S_{R’}=\{x’:\exists y’ \text{ s.t. }(x’,y’)\in R’\}$
      • for every $x\in S_R$ and $y’\in R’(f(x))$ it holds that $(x,g(x,y’)) \in R$,where $R’(x’)=\{y’ : (x’,y’)\in R’\}$
    • For search problem; $f$ transforms the problem instance (and preserves the existence of solutions), $g$ transfroms the solutions.
  • Computationally equivalent(ce): two problems are educible to one another(not class!)
    • Finding a “maximization” is computationally equivalent to finding a solution that excceding a threshold(but may not in the same class).
  • Self-Reducibility of Search Problems: “$R$ is efficiently solvable” $\approx_{ce}$ “$S_R$ is efficiently solvable($S_R = \{x: \exists y $ s.t. $(x,y)\in R\}$)”
    • An important set:$S’_R=\{(x,y’): \exists y’ $ s.t $(x,y’y’’)\in R\}$; $S’_R$ relates $R$ to $S_R$( for some $R$)
    • if $R \in NPC$, $R \rightarrow_r S’_R \rightarrow_r S_R$

NP-Completeness

  • NPC:
    • Karp reduction
    • NP + NP hard
  • Important: NPC (decision) problem exists; Cook theorem
    • “Encoding NP problem”:
      • $R_u=\{(\langle M,x,1^t\rangle,y) | M$ accepts $(x,y)$ in $t$ steps $\}$
      • $S_u=\{\bar{x}=\langle M,x,1^t \rangle | \exists y$ s.t. $(\bar{x},y) \in R_u \}$
      • $S_u \in NP$: decide $\bar{x} \in S_u$ using Universal TM.
      • $x \rightarrow f(x):=\langle M_R ,x,1^{t_R(|x|+p_R(|x|))}\rangle$: $f$ is poly-computable(Karp reduction)
    • Cook theorem: Encoding NP problem $S$ to SAT
      • problem instance $x$ of $S$ $\rightarrow $ circuit $C_x$ s.t. $C_x(y)=1 \iff M_R$ accepts $(x,y)$ within $t_R(|x| + p_R(|x|))$ steps. ($R$ is the witness relation of $S$)
      • $x \in S \iff \exists y\in{0,1}^{p_R(|x|)}$ s.t. $C_x(y)=1 (C_x \in CSAT)$
      • $CSAT \rightarrow_r SAT$
  • $NPI$: $NP \rightarrow_\text{spilt} \{P, NPI, NPC\}$(maybe)
    • $\mathcal{NP \ne P} \Rightarrow \exists T\in \mathcal{NP} \text{ \ } \mathcal{P} $ s.t. some sets in $\mathcal{NP}$ are not Cook-reducible to $T$.

Three Relatively Advanced Topics

  • Promise problem:
    • Search problems with promise P: just solve the problem instances of P(for example, $P = S_R = \{x:\exists y \text{ s.t. } (x,y) \in R \}$; candid search problem)
    • Decision problems with promise P: $P = S_{yes} \cup S_{no} $
    • restriction of a computational problem to a subset of the instances.
    • Avoiding promise problem; Efficiently recognizable…
  • Optimal search algorithms for NP:
    • Th 2.33: There exists an optimal algorithm for solving the candid search problem of any relation in $\mathcal{PC}$.
  • co$\mathcal{NP}$:
    • co$\mathcal{C}= \{\{0,1\}^* \text{\ } S: S \in \mathcal{C} \}$
      • $\mathcal{NP \ne coNP}$ and set $S\in \mathcal{NP}$ that every set in $\mathcal{NP}$ is Karp-reducible to $S$. $\Rightarrow$ co$S \notin \mathcal{NP}$.
      • $\forall S \in \mathcal{NP}$ can be Cook-reduced to some set in $\mathcal{NP \cap coNP}$, then $\mathcal{NP=coNP}$
    • $\mathcal{P =NP \Rightarrow NP = coNP} $ but $\mathcal{P\ne NP \nRightarrow NP \ne coNP}$
    • A misunderstanding of $NTM,\mathcal{NP}$ and $\mathcal{coNP}$
